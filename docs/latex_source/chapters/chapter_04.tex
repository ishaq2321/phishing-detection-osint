\section{Phishing Attack Landscape}\label{phishing-attack-landscape}

\subsection{Definition and
Characteristics}\label{definition-and-characteristics}

Phishing is a form of cybercrime in which attackers impersonate
legitimate entities to fraudulently obtain sensitive information from
unsuspecting victims. The term ``phishing'' is derived from the analogy
of ``fishing'' for victims using deceptive ``bait'' (fake emails,
websites, or messages) \cite{hong2012}. Unlike traditional hacking techniques
that exploit technical vulnerabilities in software or networks, phishing
exploits the human element---the tendency to trust familiar brands,
authority figures, and urgent requests.

A typical phishing attack follows this workflow:

\begin{enumerate}

\item
  \textbf{Reconnaissance:} The attacker identifies a target audience
  (e.g., customers of a specific bank, employees of a company, or
  general internet users).
\item
  \textbf{Bait Creation:} The attacker crafts a deceptive message
  (email, SMS, social media post) or creates a fake website that mimics
  a trusted entity (e.g., PayPal, Microsoft, a bank).
\item
  \textbf{Distribution:} The bait is distributed to potential victims
  via email spam, SMS (smishing), social media, or malicious
  advertisements.
\item
  \textbf{Exploitation:} Victims who click on links or open attachments
  are directed to a fake login page, malware download, or credential
  harvesting form.
\item
  \textbf{Data Harvesting:} The attacker collects entered credentials,
  credit card numbers, or other sensitive data.
\item
  \textbf{Monetization:} Stolen data is used for financial fraud,
  identity theft, account takeover, or sold on dark web marketplaces.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Key characteristics of phishing attacks include:}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Social Engineering:} Exploiting psychological manipulation
  (urgency, fear, curiosity, greed) rather than technical
  vulnerabilities.
\item
  \textbf{Brand Impersonation:} Mimicking the visual identity (logos,
  color schemes, language) of trusted organizations.
\item
  \textbf{URL Obfuscation:} Using look-alike domains (typosquatting),
  subdomains, URL shorteners, or IP addresses to disguise malicious
  links.
\item
  \textbf{Short Lifespan:} Phishing sites typically remain active for
  only hours to days before being taken down or abandoned.
\item
  \textbf{Low Barrier to Entry:} Phishing kits (pre-packaged templates)
  are widely available on underground forums, enabling non-technical
  attackers to launch campaigns.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Social Engineering
Tactics}\label{social-engineering-tactics}

Phishing attacks succeed primarily through psychological manipulation
rather than technical sophistication. Attackers exploit well-documented
cognitive biases and emotional triggers:

\textbf{1. Urgency and Time Pressure} Messages create artificial
deadlines to bypass rational decision-making: - ``Your account will be
closed in 24 hours unless you verify immediately.'' - ``Urgent:
Unauthorized transaction detected. Click here now.''

\textbf{2. Authority and Trust} Impersonating figures of authority or
trusted brands: - Fake emails from IT departments, CEOs, government
agencies - Visual mimicry of brand logos, color schemes, email
signatures

\textbf{3. Fear and Threat} Inducing panic through threats of negative
consequences: - ``Your account has been compromised.'' - ``Legal action
will be taken unless you respond.''

\textbf{4. Curiosity and Greed} Exploiting natural curiosity or desire
for rewards: - ``You've won a lottery!'' - ``See who viewed your
profile.''

\textbf{5. Familiarity and Context} Leveraging recent events or personal
information (spear phishing): - Referencing recent online purchases -
Mentioning colleagues' names (obtained via LinkedIn)

\textbf{6. Scarcity} Creating perception of limited availability: -
``Only 3 spots left for this exclusive offer.'' - ``Claim your prize
before midnight.''

These tactics are often combined in multi-layered attacks. For example,
a spear phishing email might impersonate a trusted colleague
(authority), reference a recent project (familiarity), and demand
immediate action on a ``confidential'' document (urgency + curiosity).

\subsection{Evolution and Trends}\label{evolution-and-trends}

Phishing attacks have evolved significantly since the first documented
case in the mid-1990s (targeting AOL users) \cite{jakobsson2006}:

\textbf{1990s-2000s: Early Email Phishing} - Mass spam campaigns with
obvious grammatical errors - Generic greetings (``Dear Customer'') - Low
sophistication, high volume

\textbf{2000s-2010s: Professionalization} - Phishing kits democratize
attacks - Improved visual mimicry of brands - Introduction of HTTPS on
phishing sites (70\% of phishing sites now use HTTPS \cite{phishlabs2023})

\textbf{2010s-2020s: Targeted and Multi-Vector} - Rise of spear phishing
and business email compromise (BEC) - Mobile phishing (smishing)
exploits smaller screens and touch interfaces - Cryptocurrency scams and
COVID-19 pandemic-themed attacks

\textbf{2020s-Present: AI-Augmented and Polymorphic} - Large Language
Models (LLMs) generate convincing phishing emails without grammatical
errors - Deepfake voice and video used in vishing attacks - Polymorphic
phishing sites that change content/URL to evade detection - Zero-day
phishing domains registered in real-time

Recent trends highlight the inadequacy of static blacklist-based
defenses, motivating the development of proactive, ML-based detection
systems.

\section{Traditional Detection
Methods}\label{traditional-detection-methods}

\subsection{Blacklist-Based
Approaches}\label{blacklist-based-approaches}

Blacklist-based detection systems maintain databases of known phishing
URLs, domains, or email senders. When a user encounters a URL, it is
checked against the blacklist; a match triggers a warning or block.

\textbf{Major Blacklist Services:}

\begin{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Google Safe Browsing:} Protects over 4 billion devices by
  sharing threat intelligence with browsers (Chrome, Firefox, Safari)
  \cite{google_safe_browsing}.
\item
  \textbf{PhishTank:} Community-driven database with over 8 million
  verified phishing URLs submitted by volunteers \cite{phishtank}.
\item
  \textbf{OpenPhish:} Automated phishing detection service providing
  real-time feeds \cite{openphish}.
\item
  \textbf{APWG eCrime Exchange (eCX):} Industry consortium sharing
  phishing data among financial institutions \cite{apwg_ecx}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Advantages:} - High precision (few false positives) when
blacklist is accurate - Low computational overhead (simple hash table
lookup) - Well-integrated into browsers and email clients

\textbf{Limitations:} - \textbf{Zero-Hour Vulnerability:} Cannot detect
new phishing sites until reported, verified, and added to blacklist
(delay of hours to days) - \textbf{Short-Lived Phishing Sites:} 50\% of
phishing URLs remain active for $<$12 hours \cite{ramesh2015_ch4}, often
disappearing before blacklisting - \textbf{Evasion Techniques:}
Attackers use URL shorteners, redirects, or dynamic URL generation to
bypass blacklists - \textbf{Scalability:} Millions of new phishing URLs
daily overwhelm manual verification processes

\subsection{Heuristic Rule-Based
Systems}\label{heuristic-rule-based-systems}

Heuristic systems define handcrafted rules based on common phishing
patterns:

\textbf{URL-Based Heuristics:} - URL contains IP address instead of
domain name → suspicious - Excessive subdomains ($>$3) →
suspicious - Domain contains brand name + suspicious TLD (e.g.,
\texttt{paypal-verify.tk}) → suspicious - URL length $>$75
characters → suspicious

\textbf{Content-Based Heuristics:} - HTML form requesting
password/credit card → high risk - Page contains multiple external links
to different domains → suspicious - Visual similarity to known brand
(logo/color matching) → potential phishing

\textbf{Email-Based Heuristics:} - Sender domain doesn't match ``From''
display name - Email contains urgent language (``act now'', ``verify
immediately'') - Links in email point to domain different from sender
domain

\textbf{Advantages:} - Can detect zero-day phishing attempts (not
reliant on blacklists) - Explainable decisions (specific rule triggered)
- Fast execution (deterministic rule evaluation)

\textbf{Limitations:} - \textbf{High False Positive Rate:} Legitimate
sites occasionally trigger heuristics (e.g., long URLs with tracking
parameters) - \textbf{Brittleness:} Attackers adapt to evade specific
rules (e.g., keeping URL length $<$75 characters) - \textbf{Manual
Tuning Required:} Rules must be continuously updated as attack patterns
evolve - \textbf{Limited Generalization:} Cannot adapt to novel attack
patterns not covered by existing rules

\subsection{Visual Similarity
Detection}\label{visual-similarity-detection}

Visual similarity techniques compare the rendered appearance of a
suspicious webpage with legitimate brand pages using computer vision:

\textbf{Techniques:} - \textbf{Image Hashing:} Perceptual hashing
(pHash) to detect visually similar logos or layouts - \textbf{OCR + Text
Matching:} Extracting text from screenshots and comparing to known
brands - \textbf{DOM Tree Similarity:} Comparing HTML structure and CSS
styles

\textbf{Advantages:} - Detects sophisticated brand impersonation (visual
mimicry) - Language-agnostic (works regardless of text content)

\textbf{Limitations:} - \textbf{Computationally Expensive:} Rendering
and analyzing page screenshots is slow - \textbf{Evasion via Minor
Changes:} Attackers introduce small visual variations to bypass
similarity thresholds - \textbf{False Positives:} Legitimate resellers
or affiliates may use brand logos legally

\textbf{Overarching Limitations:} All traditional methods share common

\section{Machine Learning for Phishing
Detection}\label{machine-learning-for-phishing-detection}

\subsection{Feature-Based
Classification}\label{feature-based-classification}

Feature-based ML approaches treat phishing detection as a supervised
binary classification problem: given a URL or email, predict whether it
is phishing (class 1) or legitimate (class 0).

\textbf{General Workflow:} 1. \textbf{Data Collection:} Gather labeled
datasets of phishing (e.g., PhishTank) and legitimate (e.g., Alexa Top
Sites) URLs. 2. \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Extract quantifiable
features from URLs, email headers, page content, etc. 3. \textbf{Model
Training:} Train a classifier (Random Forest, SVM, Logistic Regression,
XGBoost) on labeled data. 4. \textbf{Evaluation:} Assess performance on
held-out test set using accuracy, precision, recall, F1, AUC-ROC. 5.
\textbf{Deployment:} Integrate trained model into detection pipeline for
real-time inference.

\textbf{Common Feature Categories:}

\textbf{URL Structural Features (15-20 features):} - Length metrics: URL
length, domain length, path length - Character statistics: digit ratio,
special character count, entropy - Structural indicators: has IP
address, has @ symbol, subdomain count, path depth - Protocol: HTTPS vs
HTTP, presence of port number - TLD (Top-Level Domain): suspicious TLDs
(.tk, .ml, .ga)

\textbf{Content-Based Features (10-15 features):} - Number of external
links, iframes, pop-ups - Presence of login forms requesting credentials
- JavaScript obfuscation patterns - Page rank or Alexa rank (if
available)

\textbf{Domain-Based Features (5-10 features):} - Domain age (from
WHOIS) - Domain registration length (short-term registrations are
suspicious) - DNS record validity (A, MX, NS records) - WHOIS privacy
protection enabled/disabled

\textbf{Reputation Features (3-5 features):} - Google Safe Browsing
status - VirusTotal detection count - Presence in spam databases

\textbf{Key Observations:} - \textbf{Ensemble Methods Dominate:} Random
Forest, XGBoost, and LightGBM consistently achieve $>$96\%
accuracy due to their ability to model non-linear feature interactions.
- \textbf{Diminishing Returns from Feature Explosion:} Studies with 100+

\textbf{Dataset Size Matters:} Larger datasets ($>$50,000
samples) tend to produce more robust models with better generalization.
- \textbf{OSINT Underutilized:} Most studies focus on static URL/content
features; few integrate real-time OSINT (domain age, DNS, reputation),
representing a gap this thesis addresses.

\subsection{Deep Learning
Approaches}\label{deep-learning-approaches}

Recent work explores deep neural networks for phishing detection,
particularly for content and visual analysis:

\textbf{Text-Based Deep Learning:} - \textbf{Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNNs/LSTMs):} Process email text or URL character sequences to detect
patterns \cite{aljofey2020}. - \textbf{Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs):}
Applied to URL strings treated as 1D sequences \cite{li2019}. -
\textbf{Transformers (BERT, GPT):} Fine-tuned on phishing email corpora
for semantic understanding \cite{hiransha2022}.

\textbf{Visual Deep Learning:} - \textbf{CNNs for Screenshot
Classification:} Train CNNs on rendered page screenshots to detect
visual brand impersonation \cite{jain2018}. - \textbf{Siamese Networks:} Learn
visual similarity between suspicious pages and known legitimate brand
pages \cite{dhamija2006}.

\textbf{Advantages:} - Automatic feature learning (no manual feature
engineering) - High capacity for complex pattern recognition -
State-of-the-art results on large datasets ($>$1M samples)

\textbf{Limitations:} - \textbf{Data Hungry:} Require massive labeled
datasets (often $>$100k samples for deep models) -
\textbf{Black-Box Nature:} Lack of interpretability hinders trust and
debugging - \textbf{Computational Cost:} Training and inference are
resource-intensive (GPU required) - \textbf{Overfitting Risk:} Prone to
memorizing training data patterns without generalizing

For this thesis, we opt for XGBoost (gradient boosting trees) over deep
learning due to: 1. Superior performance on tabular data (structured
features) 2. Built-in explainability via SHAP TreeExplainer 3. Faster
training and inference 4. Lower computational requirements (CPU-only)

\subsection{Explainable AI for Phishing
Detection}\label{explainable-ai-for-phishing-detection}

A critical limitation of many ML-based phishing detectors is their
``black-box'' nature: they provide predictions without explaining why.
This undermines user trust and hinders security education.

\textbf{Explainability Techniques:}

\textbf{1. Feature Importance (Global Explanations):} - \textbf{Gini
Importance (Random Forest):} Measures feature contribution to tree
splits. - \textbf{SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations):} Game-theoretic
approach assigning each feature a contribution score \cite{lundberg2017}. -
\textbf{LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations):}
Approximates black-box model locally with interpretable model \cite{ribeiro2016}.

\textbf{2. Decision Visualization (Instance-Level Explanations):} -
\textbf{SHAP Waterfall Plots:} Show how each feature pushes prediction
toward phishing/legitimate for a specific URL. - \textbf{Decision Trees
(if model is tree-based):} Visualize decision path.

\textbf{3. Rule Extraction:} - Extract human-readable IF-THEN rules from
trained models (e.g., using RIPPER algorithm).

\textbf{PhishGuard Implementation:} We integrate SHAP TreeExplainer to
provide per-prediction feature importance scores and visualizations,
enabling users to understand exactly why a URL was flagged (e.g.,
``domain age $<$30 days contributed +0.15 to phishing score'').

\subsection{WHOIS Domain Registration
Data}\label{whois-domain-registration-data}

WHOIS is a query/response protocol providing information about domain
name registrations (RFC 3912) \cite{daigle2004}.

\textbf{Relevant WHOIS Data Points:}

\begin{enumerate}

\item
  \textbf{Domain Age (Creation Date):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Domains registered $<$30 days ago
    are highly suspicious (attackers register fresh domains to evade
    blacklists).
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:}
    \texttt{domainAgeDays\ =\ (current\_date\ -\ creation\_date).days}
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Registrar:}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Cheap or anonymous registrars (e.g.,
    Freenom offering free .tk, .ml, .ga domains) are favored by
    attackers.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Binary flag \texttt{isCheapRegistrar}
    or categorical encoding.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Privacy Protection:}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} WHOIS privacy services hide registrant
    identity; legitimate businesses typically display contact info.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} Binary flag \texttt{isPrivate}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{Registration Length:}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Short-term registrations (1 year or
    less) are suspicious; legitimate businesses often register for
    multiple years.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{registrationLengthYears}.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Challenges:} - \textbf{Privacy Masking:} GDPR regulations and
privacy services obscure registrant data, reducing WHOIS utility. -
\textbf{Rate Limiting:} Public WHOIS servers impose query rate limits. -
\textbf{Parsing Inconsistency:} WHOIS responses vary by registrar,
requiring robust parsing logic.

\subsection{DNS Infrastructure
Analysis}\label{dns-infrastructure-analysis}

Domain Name System (DNS) records provide insights into domain
infrastructure and configuration.

\textbf{Relevant DNS Record Types:}

\begin{enumerate}

\item
  \textbf{A / AAAA Records (IP Address):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} IP geolocation in high-risk countries,
    shared hosting with known malicious sites.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{hasValidDns} (boolean),
    \texttt{ipCountryCode}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{MX Records (Mail Servers):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Absence of MX records suggests domain
    isn't configured for email (unusual for business sites).
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{hasValidMx} (boolean),
    \texttt{mxRecordCount}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{NS Records (Name Servers):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Use of free DNS services (e.g.,
    afraid.org) or frequent nameserver changes.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{nameServerProvider}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{TXT Records (SPF, DMARC, DKIM):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator:} Lack of email authentication records
    suggests domain isn't used for legitimate email.
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{hasSpf}, \texttt{hasDmarc}.
  \end{itemize}
\item
  \textbf{CNAME Records (Aliases):}

  \begin{itemize}
    \item
    \textbf{Phishing Indicator (Positive):} CDN usage (Cloudflare,
    Akamai) indicates infrastructure investment (legitimate sites).
  \item
    \textbf{Feature Engineering:} \texttt{usesCdn} (boolean).
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{PhishGuard DNS Features:} We extract four DNS-derived features:
- \texttt{hasValidMx}: Boolean indicating presence of MX records -
\texttt{usesCdn}: Boolean indicating CDN usage (via CNAME/NS analysis) -
\texttt{dnsRecordCount}: Total number of DNS records -
\texttt{hasValidDns}: Boolean indicating successful A record resolution

\subsection{Reputation Databases}\label{reputation-databases}

Reputation services aggregate threat intelligence from multiple sources.

\textbf{1. VirusTotal:} - Submits URLs to 70+ security scanners
(antivirus, blacklists, heuristics). - Returns count of scanners
flagging URL as malicious. - \textbf{Feature Engineering:}
\texttt{virusTotalDetections\ /\ totalScanners} → normalized reputation
score.

\textbf{2. AbuseIPDB:} - Community-driven IP address abuse reporting
database. - Provides ``abuse confidence score'' (0-100\%) based on
report volume and recency. - \textbf{Feature Engineering:}
\texttt{abuseConfidenceScore} (0-1 normalized).

\textbf{3. Google Safe Browsing:} - Binary classification (safe
vs.~malware/phishing/unwanted software). - \textbf{Feature Engineering:}
\texttt{isGoogleBlacklisted} (boolean).

\textbf{Challenges:} - \textbf{API Rate Limits:} Free tiers restrict
queries (e.g., VirusTotal: 4 requests/min). - \textbf{Latency:} External
API calls add 200-1000ms per URL. - \textbf{Privacy Concerns:}
Submitting URLs to third parties may leak user data.

\textbf{PhishGuard Implementation:} We use VirusTotal and AbuseIPDB APIs
with graceful degradation: if APIs fail or rate limits are exceeded, the
model proceeds with URL-only features (OSINT features set to neutral
values).

\section{Gap Analysis and Research
Positioning}\label{gap-analysis-and-research-positioning}

\subsection{Identified Gaps in Existing
Work}\label{identified-gaps-in-existing-work}

Despite extensive research on phishing detection, several gaps remain:

\textbf{G1. Limited OSINT Integration in ML Models:} Most ML-based
studies focus on static URL/content features extracted at analysis time.
Few integrate real-time OSINT (WHOIS, DNS, reputation) that provides
external context about domain infrastructure. Studies that do
incorporate OSINT (e.g., domain age) often use small feature sets
($<$5 OSINT features) and don't rigorously evaluate their
contribution via ablation studies.

\textbf{G2. Lack of Explainability:} High-accuracy models (especially
deep learning) often function as black boxes. Few studies integrate
explainability frameworks (SHAP, LIME) to provide users with
understandable reasons for classifications.

\textbf{G3. End-to-End System Absence:} Academic research typically
focuses on model training and evaluation but stops short of building
production-ready systems with full-stack web interfaces, API endpoints,
comprehensive testing, and deployment.

\textbf{G4. Narrow Input Modality:} Most tools handle only URLs or only
emails, requiring users to manually extract and format data. Few support
multi-modal input (URL, email with subject/sender, free-text) with
automatic content-type detection.

\textbf{G5. Insufficient Dataset Scale:} Many studies use datasets of
10,000-50,000 URLs. Larger datasets ($>$100,000) improve model
generalization but are underutilized in the literature.
